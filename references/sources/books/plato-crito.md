**Crito.** Yes, certainly. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** The dawn is breaking. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** I wonder the keeper of the prison would let you [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)in. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** And are you only just come? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** No, I came some time ago. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Then why did you sit and say nothing, instead of awakening [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)me at once? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Why, indeed, Socrates, I myself would rather not have [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)all this sleeplessness and sorrow. But I have been wondering at your peaceful [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)slumbers, and that was the reason why I did not awaken you, because I wanted [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)you to be out of pain. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Why, Crito, when a man has reached my age he ought [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)not to be repining at the prospect of death. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** And yet other old men find themselves in similar misfortunes, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)and age does not prevent them from repining. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** That may be. But you have not told me why you come [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)at this early hour. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** I come to bring you a message which is sad and painful; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)not, as I believe, to yourself but to all of us who are your friends, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)saddest of all to me. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** What! I suppose that the ship has come from Delos, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)on the arrival of which I am to die? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** No, the ship has not actually arrived, but she will [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)probably be here to-day, as persons who have come from Sunium tell me that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)they have left her there; and therefore to-morrow, Socrates, will be the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)last day of your life. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Very well, Crito; if such is the will of God, I am [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)willing; but my belief is that there will be a delay of a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)day. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Why do you say this? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** I will tell you. I am to die on the day after the arrival [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)of the ship? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Yes; that is what the authorities [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)say. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** But I do not think that the ship will be here until [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)to-morrow; this I gather from a vision which I had last night, or rather [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)only just now, when you fortunately allowed me to sleep. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** And what was the nature of the vision? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** There came to me the likeness of a woman, fair and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)comely, clothed in white raiment, who called to me and said: O [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)Socrates- [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** What a singular dream, Socrates! [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** There can be no doubt about the meaning Crito, I [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)think. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Yes: the meaning is only too clear. But, O! my beloved [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)Socrates, let me entreat you once more to take my advice and escape. Now, can there be a worse disgrace than this- that I should [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)be thought to value money more than the life of a friend? For the many [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)will not be persuaded that I wanted you to escape, and that you [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)refused. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** But why, my dear Crito, should we care about the opinion [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)of the many? Good men, and they are the only persons who are worth considering, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)will think of these things truly as they happened. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** But do you see. Socrates, that the opinion of the many [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)must be regarded, as is evident in your own case, because they can do the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)very greatest evil to anyone who has lost their good [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)opinion? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** I only wish, Crito, that they could; for then they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)could also do the greatest good, and that would be well. But the truth [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)is, that they can do neither good nor evil: they cannot make a man wise [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)or make him foolish; and whatever they do is the result of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)chance. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Well, I will not dispute about that; but please to tell [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)me, Socrates, whether you are not acting out of regard to me and your other [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)friends: are you not afraid that if you escape hence we may get into trouble [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)with the informers for having stolen you away, and lose either the whole [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)or a great part of our property; or that even a worse evil may happen to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)us? Now, if this is your fear, be at ease; for in order to save you, we [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)ought surely to run this or even a greater risk; be persuaded, then, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)do as I say. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Yes, Crito, that is one fear which you mention, but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)by no means the only one. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Fear not. There are persons who at no great cost are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)willing to save you and bring you out of prison; and as for the informers, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)you may observe that they are far from being exorbitant in their demands; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)a little money will satisfy them. My means, which, as I am sure, are ample, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)are at your service, and if you have a scruple about spending all mine, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)here are strangers who will give you the use of theirs; and one of them, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)Simmias the Theban, has brought a sum of money for this very purpose; and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)Cebes and many others are willing to spend their money too. I say, therefore, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)do not on that account hesitate about making your escape, and do not say, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)as you did in the court, that you will have a difficulty in knowing what [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)to do with yourself if you escape. For men will love you in other places [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)to which you may go, and not in Athens only; there are friends of mine [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)in Thessaly, if you like to go to them, who will value and protect you, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)and no Thessalian will give you any trouble. Nor can I think that you are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)justified, Socrates, in betraying your own life when you might be saved; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)this is playing into the hands of your enemies and destroyers; and moreover [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)I should say that you were betraying your children; for you might bring [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)them up and educate them; instead of which you go away and leave them, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)and they will have to take their chance; and if they do not meet with the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)usual fate of orphans, there will be small thanks to you. No man should [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)bring children into the world who is unwilling to persevere to the end [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)in their nurture and education. But you are choosing the easier part, as [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)I think, not the better and manlier, which would rather have become one [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)who professes virtue in all his actions, like yourself. And, indeed, I [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)am ashamed not only of you, but of us who are your friends, when I reflect [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)that this entire business of yours will be attributed to our want of courage. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)The trial need never have come on, or might have been brought to another [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)issue; and the end of all, which is the crowning absurdity, will seem to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)have been permitted by us, through cowardice and baseness, who might have [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)saved you, as you might have saved yourself, if we had been good for anything [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)(for there was no difficulty in escaping); and we did not see how disgraceful, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)Socrates, and also miserable all this will be to us as well as to you. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)Make your mind up then, or rather have your mind already made up, for the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)time of deliberation is over, and there is only one thing to be done, which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)must be done, if at all, this very night, and which any delay will render [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)all but impossible; I beseech you therefore, Socrates, to be persuaded [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)by me, and to do as I say. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Dear Crito, your zeal is invaluable, if a right one; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)but if wrong, the greater the zeal the greater the evil; and therefore [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)we ought to consider whether these things shall be done or not. For I am [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)and always have been one of those natures who must be guided by reason, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)whatever the reason may be which upon reflection appears to me to be the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)best; and now that this fortune has come upon me, I cannot put away the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)reasons which I have before given: the principles which I have hitherto [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)honored and revered I still honor, and unless we can find other and better [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)principles on the instant, I am certain not to agree with you; no, not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)even if the power of the multitude could inflict many more imprisonments, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)confiscations, deaths, frightening us like children with hobgoblin terrors. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)But what will be the fairest way of considering the question? Shall I return [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)to your old argument about the opinions of men, some of which are to be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)regarded, and others, as we were saying, are not to be regarded? Now were [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)we right in maintaining this before I was condemned? And has the argument [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)which was once good now proved to be talk for the sake of talking; in fact [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)an amusement only, and altogether vanity? That is what I want to consider [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)with your help, Crito: whether, under my present circumstances, the argument [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)appears to be in any way different or not; and is to be allowed by me or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)disallowed. That argument, which, as I believe, is maintained by many who [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)assume to be authorities, was to the effect, as I was saying, that the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)opinions of some men are to be regarded, and of other men not to be regarded. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)Now you, Crito, are a disinterested person who are not going to die to-morrow- [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)at least, there is no human probability of this, and you are therefore [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)not liable to be deceived by the circumstances in which you are placed. I ask you whether I was right [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)in maintaining this? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Certainly. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** The good are to be regarded, and not the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)bad? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Yes. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** And the opinions of the wise are good, and the opinions [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)of the unwise are evil? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** And what was said about another matter? Was the disciple [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)in gymnastics supposed to attend to the praise and blame and opinion of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)every man, or of one man only- his physician or trainer, whoever that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)was? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Of one man only. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** And he ought to fear the censure and welcome the praise [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)of that one only, and not of the many? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** That is clear. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** And he ought to live and train, and eat and drink in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)the way which seems good to his single master who has understanding, rather [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)than according to the opinion of all other men put together? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** True. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** And if he disobeys and disregards the opinion and approval [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)of the one, and regards the opinion of the many who have no understanding, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)will he not suffer evil? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Certainly he will. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** And what will the evil be, whither tending and what [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)affcting, in the disobedient person? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Very good; and is not this true, Crito, of other things [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)which we need not separately enumerate? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Certainly there is, Socrates. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Take a parallel instance; if, acting under the advice [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)of men who have no understanding, we destroy that which is improvable by [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)health and deteriorated by disease- when that has been destroyed, I say, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)would life be worth having? **Soc.** Could we live, having an evil and corrupted [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)body? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Certainly not. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** And will life be worth having, if that higher part [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)of man be depraved, which is improved by justice and deteriorated by injustice? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)Do we suppose that principle, whatever it may be in man, which has to do [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)with justice and injustice, to be inferior to the body? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** More honored, then? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Far more honored. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Then, my friend, we must not regard what the many say [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)of us: but what he, the one man who has understanding of just and unjust, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)will say, and what the truth will say. **Cr.** Yes, Socrates; that will clearly be the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)answer. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** That is true; but still I find with surprise that the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)old argument is, as I conceive, unshaken as ever. And I should like to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)know Whether I may say the same of another proposition- that not life, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)but a good life, is to be chiefly valued? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Yes, that also remains. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** And a good life is equivalent to a just and honorable [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)one- that holds also? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Yes, that holds. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** From these premises I proceed to argue the question [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)whether I ought or ought not to try to escape without the consent of the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)Athenians: and if I am clearly right in escaping, then I will make the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)attempt; but if not, I will abstain. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** I think that you are right, Socrates; how then shall [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)we proceed? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Let us consider the matter together, and do you either [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)refute me if you can, and I will be convinced; or else cease, my dear friend, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)from repeating to me that I ought to escape against the wishes of the Athenians: [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)for I am extremely desirous to be persuaded by you, but not against my [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)own better judgment. And now please to consider my first position, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)do your best to answer me. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** I will do my best. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Are we to say that we are never intentionally to do [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)wrong, or that in one way we ought and in another way we ought not to do [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)wrong, or is doing wrong always evil and dishonorable, as I was just now [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)saying, and as has been already acknowledged by us? Are all our former [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)admissions which were made within a few days to be thrown away? And have [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)we, at our age, been earnestly discoursing with one another all our life [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)long only to discover that we are no better than children? Or are we to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)rest assured, in spite of the opinion of the many, and in spite of consequences [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)whether better or worse, of the truth of what was then said, that injustice [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)is always an evil and dishonor to him who acts unjustly? Shall we affirm [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)that? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Then we must do no wrong? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Nor when injured injure in return, as the many imagine; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)for we must injure no one at all? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Clearly not. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Again, Crito, may we do evil? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Surely not, Socrates. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** And what of doing evil in return for evil, which is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)the morality of the many-is that just or not? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Not just. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** For doing evil to another is the same as injuring [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)him? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Very true. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Then we ought not to retaliate or render evil for evil [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)to anyone, whatever evil we may have suffered from him. But I would have [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)you consider, Crito, whether you really mean what you are saying. For this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)opinion has never been held, and never will be held, by any considerable [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)number of persons; and those who are agreed and those who are not agreed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)upon this point have no common ground, and can only despise one another, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)when they see how widely they differ. Tell me, then, whether you agree [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)with and assent to my first principle, that neither injury nor retaliation [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)nor warding off evil by evil is ever right. And shall that be the premise [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)of our agreement? Or do you decline and dissent from this? For this has [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)been of old and is still my opinion; but, if you are of another opinion, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)let me hear what you have to say. If, however, you remain of the same mind [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)as formerly, I will proceed to the next step. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** You may proceed, for I have not changed my [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)mind. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Then I will proceed to the next step, which may be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)put in the form of a question: Ought a man to do what he admits to be right, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)or ought he to betray the right? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** He ought to do what he thinks right. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** But if this is true, what is the application? In leaving [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)the prison against the will of the Athenians, do I wrong any? or rather [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)do I not wrong those whom I ought least to wrong? Do I not desert the principles [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)which were acknowledged by us to be just? What do you [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)say? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** I cannot tell, Socrates, for I do not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)know. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Then consider the matter in this way: Imagine that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)I am about to play truant (you may call the proceeding by any name which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)you like), and the laws and the government come and interrogate me: "Tell [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)us, Socrates," they say; "what are you about? are you going by an act of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)yours to overturn us- the laws and the whole State, as far as in you lies? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)Do you imagine that a State can subsist and not be overthrown, in which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)the decisions of law have no power, but are set aside and overthrown by [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)individuals?" What will be our answer, Crito, to these and the like words? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)Anyone, and especially a clever rhetorician, will have a good deal to urge [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)about the evil of setting aside the law which requires a sentence to be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)carried out; and we might reply, "Yes; but the State has injured us and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)given an unjust sentence." Suppose I say that? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** Very good, Socrates. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

Soc. "And was that our agreement with you?" the law would sar, "or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)were you to abide by the sentence of the State?" And if I were to express [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)astonishment at their saying this, the law would probably add: "Answer, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)Socrates, instead of opening your eyes: you are in the habit of asking [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)and answering questions. Tell us what complaint you have to make against [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)us which justifies you in attempting to destroy us and the State? In the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)first place did we not bring you into existence? Your father married your [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)mother by our aid and begat you. Say whether you have any objection to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)urge against those of us who regulate marriage?" None, I should reply. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)"Or against those of us who regulate the system of nurture and education [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)of children in which you were trained? Were not the laws, who have the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)charge of this, right in commanding your father to train you in music and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)gymnastic?" Right, I should reply. "Well, then, since you were brought [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)into the world and nurtured and educated by us, can you deny in the first [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)place that you are our child and slave, as your fathers were before you? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)And if this is true you are not on equal terms with us; nor can you think [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)that you have a right to do to us what we are doing to you. Would you have [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)any right to strike or revile or do any other evil to a father or to your [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)master, if you had one, when you have been struck or reviled by him, or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)received some other evil at his hands?- you would not say this? And because [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)we think right to destroy you, do you think that you have any right to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)destroy us in return, and your country as far as in you lies? And will [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)you, O professor of true virtue, say that you are justified in this? Has [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)a philosopher like you failed to discover that our country is more to be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)valued and higher and holier far than mother or father or any ancestor, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)and more to be regarded in the eyes of the gods and of men of understanding? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)also to be soothed, and gently and reverently entreated when angry, even [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)more than a father, and if not persuaded, obeyed? And when we are punished [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)by her, whether with imprisonment or stripes, the punishment is to be endured [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)in silence; and if she leads us to wounds or death in battle, thither we [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)follow as is right; neither may anyone yield or retreat or leave his rank, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)but whether in battle or in a court of law, or in any other place, he must [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)do what his city and his country order him; or he must change their view [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)of what is just: and if he may do no violence to his father or mother, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)much less may he do violence to his country." What answer shall we make [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)to this, Crito? Do the laws speak truly, or do they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)not? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** I think that they do. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Then the laws will say: "Consider, Socrates, if this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)is true, that in your present attempt you are going to do us wrong. For, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)after having brought you into the world, and nurtured and educated you, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)and given you and every other citizen a share in every good that we had [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)to give, we further proclaim and give the right to every Athenian, that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)if he does not like us when he has come of age and has seen the ways of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)the city, and made our acquaintance, he may go where he pleases and take [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)his goods with him; and none of us laws will forbid him or interfere with [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)him. Any of you who does not like us and the city, and who wants to go [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)to a colony or to any other city, may go where he likes, and take his goods [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)with him. But he who has experience of the manner in which we order justice [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)and administer the State, and still remains, has entered into an implied [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)contract that he will do as we command him. And he who disobeys us is, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)as we maintain, thrice wrong: first, because in disobeying us he is disobeying [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)his parents; secondly, because we are the authors of his education; thirdly, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)because he has made an agreement with us that he will duly obey our commands; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)and he neither obeys them nor convinces us that our commands are wrong; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)and we do not rudely impose them, but give him the alternative of obeying [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)or convincing us; that is what we offer and he does neither. These are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)the sort of accusations to which, as we were saying, you, Socrates, will [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)be exposed if you accomplish your intentions; you, above all other Athenians." [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)Suppose I ask, why is this? they will justly retort upon me that I above [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)all other men have acknowledged the agreement. "There is clear proof," [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)they will say, "Socrates, that we and the city were not displeasing to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)you. Of all Athenians you have been the most constant resident in the city, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)which, as you never leave, you may be supposed to love. For you never went [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)out of the city either to see the games, except once when you went to the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)Isthmus, or to any other place unless when you were on military service; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)nor did you travel as other men do. Nor had you any curiosity to know other [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)States or their laws: your affections did not go beyond us and our State; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)we were your especial favorites, and you acquiesced in our government of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)you; and this is the State in which you begat your children, which is a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)proof of your satisfaction. Moreover, you might, if you had liked, have [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)fixed the penalty at banishment in the course of the trial-the State which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)refuses to let you go now would have let you go then. But you pretended [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)that you preferred death to exile, and that you were not grieved at death. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)And now you have forgotten these fine sentiments, and pay no respect to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)us, the laws, of whom you are the destroyer; and are doing what only a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)miserable slave would do, running away and turning your back upon the compacts [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)and agreements which you made as a citizen. And first of all answer this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)very question: Are we right in saying that you agreed to be governed according [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)to us in deed, and not in word only? Is that true or not?" How shall we [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)answer that, Crito? Must we not agree? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** There is no help, Socrates. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Then will they not say: "You, Socrates, are breaking [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)the covenants and agreements which you made with us at your leisure, not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)in any haste or under any compulsion or deception, but having had seventy [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)years to think of them, during which time you were at liberty to leave [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)the city, if we were not to your mind, or if our covenants appeared to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)you to be unfair. You had your choice, and might have gone either to Lacedaemon [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)or Crete, which you often praise for their good government, or to some [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)other Hellenic or foreign State. Whereas you, above all other Athenians, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)seemed to be so fond of the State, or, in other words, of us her laws (for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)who would like a State that has no laws?), that you never stirred out of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)her: the halt, the blind, the maimed, were not more stationary in her than [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)you were. And now you run away and forsake your agreements. Not so, Socrates, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)if you will take our advice; do not make yourself ridiculous by escaping [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)out of the city. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

"For just consider, if you transgress and err in this sort of way, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)what good will you do, either to yourself or to your friends? That your [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)friends will be driven into exile and deprived of citizenship, or will [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)lose their property, is tolerably certain; and you yourself, if you fly [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)to one of the neighboring cities, as, for example, Thebes or Megara, both [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)of which are well-governed cities, will come to them as an enemy, Socrates, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)and their government will be against you, and all patriotic citizens will [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)cast an evil eye upon you as a subverter of the laws, and you will confirm [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)in the minds of the judges the justice of their own condemnation of you. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)For he who is a corrupter of the laws is more than likely to be corrupter [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)of the young and foolish portion of mankind. Will you then flee from well-ordered [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)cities and virtuous men? and is existence worth having on these terms? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)Or will you go to them without shame, and talk to them, Socrates? And what [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)will you say to them? What you say here about virtue and justice and institutions [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)and laws being the best things among men? Would that be decent of you? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)Surely not. But if you go away from well-governed States to Crito's friends [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)in Thessaly, where there is great disorder and license, they will be charmed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)to have the tale of your escape from prison, set off with ludicrous particulars [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)of the manner in which you were wrapped in a goatskin or some other disguise, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)and metamorphosed as the fashion of runaways is- that is very likely; but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)will there be no one to remind you that in your old age you violated the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)most sacred laws from a miserable desire of a little more life? Perhaps [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)not, if you keep them in a good temper; but if they are out of temper you [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)will hear many degrading things; you will live, but how?- as the flatterer [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)of all men, and the servant of all men; and doing what?- eating and drinking [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)in Thessaly, having gone abroad in order that you may get a dinner. And [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)where will be your fine sentiments about justice and virtue then? Say that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)you wish to live for the sake of your children, that you may bring them [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)up and educate them- will you take them into Thessaly and deprive them [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)of Athenian citizenship? Is that the benefit which you would confer upon [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)them? Or are you under the impression that they will be better cared for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)and educated here if you are still alive, although absent from them; for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)that your friends will take care of them? Do you fancy that if you are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)an inhabitant of Thessaly they will take care of them, and if you are an [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)inhabitant of the other world they will not take care of them? Nay; but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)if they who call themselves friends are truly friends, they surely [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)will. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

"Listen, then, Socrates, to us who have brought you up. Think not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)of life and children first, and of justice afterwards, but of justice first, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)that you may be justified before the princes of the world below. For neither [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)will you nor any that belong to you be happier or holier or juster in this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)life, or happier in another, if you do as Crito bids. Now you depart in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)innocence, a sufferer and not a doer of evil; a victim, not of the laws, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)but of men. But if you go forth, returning evil for evil, and injury for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)injury, breaking the covenants and agreements which you have made with [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)us, and wronging those whom you ought least to wrong, that is to say, yourself, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)your friends, your country, and us, we shall be angry with you while you [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)live, and our brethren, the laws in the world below, will receive you as [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)an enemy; for they will know that you have done your best to destroy us. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)Listen, then, to us and not to Crito." [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

This is the voice which I seem to hear murmuring in my ears, like [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)the sound of the flute in the ears of the mystic; that voice, I say, is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)humming in my ears, and prevents me from hearing any other. And I know [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)that anything more which you will say will be in vain. Yet speak, if you [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)have anything to say. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Cr.** I have nothing to say, Socrates. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**Soc.** Then let me follow the intimations of the will of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)God. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/crito.html)

**THE END**
