> [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)[](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)**Socrates' Defense**[](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> How you have felt, O men of Athens, at hearing the speeches of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)my accusers, I cannot tell; but I know that their persuasive words almost [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)made me forget who I was - such was the effect of them; and yet they have [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)hardly spoken a word of truth. But many as their falsehoods were, there [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)was one of them which quite amazed me; - I mean when they told you to be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)upon your guard, and not to let yourselves be deceived by the force of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)my eloquence. They ought to have been ashamed of saying this, because they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)were sure to be detected as soon as I opened my lips and displayed my deficiency; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)they certainly did appear to be most shameless in saying this, unless by [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the force of eloquence they mean the force of truth; for then I do indeed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)admit that I am eloquent. But in how different a way from theirs! Well, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)as I was saying, they have hardly uttered a word, or not more than a word, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of truth; but you shall hear from me the whole truth: not, however, delivered [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)after their manner, in a set oration duly ornamented with words and phrases. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)No indeed! but I shall use the words and arguments which occur to me at [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the moment; for I am certain that this is right, and that at my time of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)life I ought not to be appearing before you, O men of Athens, in the character [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of a juvenile orator - let no one expect this of me. And I must beg of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)you to grant me one favor, which is this - If you hear me using the same [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)words in my defence which I have been in the habit of using, and which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)most of you may have heard in the agora, and at the tables of the money-changers, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)or anywhere else, I would ask you not to be surprised at this, and not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)to interrupt me. For I am more than seventy years of age, and this is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the first time that I have ever appeared in a court of law, and I am quite [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)a stranger to the ways of the place; and therefore I would have you regard [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)me as if I were really a stranger, whom you would excuse if he spoke in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)his native tongue, and after the fashion of his country; - that I think [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)is not an unfair request. Never mind the manner, which may or may not be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)good; but think only of the justice of my cause, and give heed to that: [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)let the judge decide justly and the speaker speak truly. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> And first, I have to reply to the older charges and to my first [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)accusers, and then I will go to the later ones. For I have had many accusers, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)who accused me of old, and their false charges have continued during many [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)years; and I am more afraid of them than of Anytus and his associates, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)who are dangerous, too, in their own way. But far more dangerous are these, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)who began when you were children, and took possession of your minds with [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)their falsehoods, telling of one Socrates, a wise man, who speculated about [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the heaven above, and searched into the earth beneath, and made the worse [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)appear the better cause. These are the accusers whom I dread; for they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)are the circulators of this rumor, and their hearers are too apt to fancy [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)that speculators of this sort do not believe in the gods. And they are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)many, and their charges against me are of ancient date, and they made them [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)in days when you were impressible - in childhood, or perhaps in youth - [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and the cause when heard went by default, for there was none to answer. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)And, hardest of all, their names I do not know and cannot tell; unless [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)in the chance of a comic poet. But the main body of these slanderers who [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)from envy and malice have wrought upon you - and there are some of them [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)who are convinced themselves, and impart their convictions to others - [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)all these, I say, are most difficult to deal with; for I cannot have them [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)up here, and examine them, and therefore I must simply fight with shadows [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)in my own defence, and examine when there is no one who answers. I will [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)ask you then to assume with me, as I was saying, that my opponents are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of two kinds - one recent, the other ancient; and I hope that you will [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)see the propriety of my answering the latter first, for these accusations [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)you heard long before the others, and much oftener. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Well, then, I will make my defence, and I will endeavor in the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)short time which is allowed to do away with this evil opinion of me which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)you have held for such a long time; and I hope I may succeed, if this be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)well for you and me, and that my words may find favor with you. But I know [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)that to accomplish this is not easy - I quite see the nature of the task. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)Let the event be as God wills: in obedience to the law I make my [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)defence. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> I will begin at the beginning, and ask what the accusation is which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)has given rise to this slander of me, and which has encouraged Meletus [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)to proceed against me. What do the slanderers say? They shall be my prosecutors, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and I will sum up their words in an affidavit. "Socrates is an evil-doer, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and a curious person, who searches into things under the earth and in heaven, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and he makes the worse appear the better cause; and he teaches the aforesaid [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)doctrines to others." That is the nature of the accusation, and that is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)what you have seen yourselves in the comedy of Aristophanes; who has introduced [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)a man whom he calls Socrates, going about and saying that he can walk in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the air, and talking a deal of nonsense concerning matters of which I do [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)not pretend to know either much or little - not that I mean to say anything [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)disparaging of anyone who is a student of natural philosophy. I should [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)be very sorry if Meletus could lay that to my charge. But the simple truth [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)is, O Athenians, that I have nothing to do with these studies. Very many [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of those here present are witnesses to the truth of this, and to them I [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)appeal. Speak then, you who have heard me, and tell your neighbors whether [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)any of you have ever known me hold forth in few words or in many upon matters [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of this sort. ... You hear their answer. And from what they say of this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)you will be able to judge of the truth of the rest. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> As little foundation is there for the report that I am a teacher, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and take money; that is no more true than the other. Although, if a man [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)is able to teach, I honor him for being paid. There is Gorgias of Leontium, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and Prodicus of Ceos, and Hippias of Elis, who go the round of the cities, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and are able to persuade the young men to leave their own citizens, by [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)whom they might be taught for nothing, and come to them, whom they not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)only pay, but are thankful if they may be allowed to pay them. There is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)actually a Parian philosopher residing in Athens, of whom I have heard; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and I came to hear of him in this way: - I met a man who has spent a world [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of money on the Sophists, Callias the son of Hipponicus, and knowing that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)he had sons, I asked him: "Callias," I said, "if your two sons were foals [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)or calves, there would be no difficulty in finding someone to put over [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)them; we should hire a trainer of horses or a farmer probably who would [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)improve and perfect them in their own proper virtue and excellence; but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)as they are human beings, whom are you thinking of placing over them? Is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)there anyone who understands human and political virtue? You must have [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)thought about this as you have sons; is there anyone?" "There is," he said. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)"Who is he?" said I, "and of what country? and what does he charge?" "Evenus [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the Parian," he replied; "he is the man, and his charge is five minae." [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)Happy is Evenus, I said to myself, if he really has this wisdom, and teaches [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)at such a modest charge. Had I the same, I should have been very proud [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and conceited; but the truth is that I have no knowledge of the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)kind. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> I dare say, Athenians, that someone among you will reply, "Why [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)is this, Socrates, and what is the origin of these accusations of you: [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)for there must have been something strange which you have been doing? All [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)this great fame and talk about you would never have arisen if you had been [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)like other men: tell us, then, why this is, as we should be sorry to judge [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)hastily of you." Now I regard this as a fair challenge, and I will endeavor [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)to explain to you the origin of this name of "wise," and of this evil fame. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)Please to attend then. And although some of you may think I am joking, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I declare that I will tell you the entire truth. Men of Athens, this reputation [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of mine has come of a certain sort of wisdom which I possess. If you ask [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)me what kind of wisdom, I reply, such wisdom as is attainable by man, for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)to that extent I am inclined to believe that I am wise; whereas the persons [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of whom I was speaking have a superhuman wisdom, which I may fail to describe, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)because I have it not myself; and he who says that I have, speaks falsely, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and is taking away my character. And here, O men of Athens, I must beg [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)you not to interrupt me, even if I seem to say something extravagant. For [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the word which I will speak is not mine. I will refer you to a witness [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)who is worthy of credit, and will tell you about my wisdom - whether I [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)have any, and of what sort - and that witness shall be the god of Delphi. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)You must have known Chaerephon; he was early a friend of mine, and also [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)a friend of yours, for he shared in the exile of the people, and returned [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)with you. Well, Chaerephon, as you know, was very impetuous in all his [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)doings, and he went to Delphi and boldly asked the oracle to tell him whether [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)- as I was saying, I must beg you not to interrupt - he asked the oracle [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)to tell him whether there was anyone wiser than I was, and the Pythian [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)prophetess answered that there was no man wiser. Chaerephon is dead himself, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)but his brother, who is in court, will confirm the truth of this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)story. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Why do I mention this? Because I am going to explain to you why [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I have such an evil name. When I heard the answer, I said to myself, What [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)can the god mean? and what is the interpretation of this riddle? for I [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)know that I have no wisdom, small or great. What can he mean when he says [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)that I am the wisest of men? And yet he is a god and cannot lie; that would [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)be against his nature. After a long consideration, I at last thought of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)a method of trying the question. I reflected that if I could only find [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)a man wiser than myself, then I might go to the god with a refutation in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)my hand. I should say to him, "Here is a man who is wiser than I am; but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)you said that I was the wisest." Accordingly I went to one who had the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)reputation of wisdom, and observed to him - his name I need not mention; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)he was a politician whom I selected for examination - and the result was [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)as follows: When I began to talk with him, I could not help thinking that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)he was not really wise, although he was thought wise by many, and wiser [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)still by himself; and I went and tried to explain to him that he thought [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)himself wise, but was not really wise; and the consequence was that he [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)hated me, and his enmity was shared by several who were present and heard [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)me. So I left him, saying to myself, as I went away: Well, although I do [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)not suppose that either of us knows anything really beautiful and good, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I am better off than he is - for he knows nothing, and thinks that he knows. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I neither know nor think that I know. In this latter particular, then, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I seem to have slightly the advantage of him. Then I went to another, who [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)had still higher philosophical pretensions, and my conclusion was exactly [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the same. I made another enemy of him, and of many others besides [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)him. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> After this I went to one man after another, being not unconscious [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of the enmity which I provoked, and I lamented and feared this: but necessity [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)was laid upon me - the word of God, I thought, ought to be considered first. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)And I said to myself, Go I must to all who appear to know, and find out [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the meaning of the oracle. And I swear to you, Athenians, by the dog I [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)swear! - for I must tell you the truth - the result of my mission was just [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)this: I found that the men most in repute were all but the most foolish; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and that some inferior men were really wiser and better. I will tell you [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the tale of my wanderings and of the "Herculean" labors, as I may call [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)them, which I endured only to find at last the oracle irrefutable. When [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I left the politicians, I went to the poets; tragic, dithyrambic, and all [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)sorts. And there, I said to myself, you will be detected; now you will [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)find out that you are more ignorant than they are. Accordingly, I took [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)them some of the most elaborate passages in their own writings, and asked [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)what was the meaning of them - thinking that they would teach me something. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)Will you believe me? I am almost ashamed to speak of this, but still I [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)must say that there is hardly a person present who would not have talked [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)better about their poetry than they did themselves. That showed me in an [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)instant that not by wisdom do poets write poetry, but by a sort of genius [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and inspiration; they are like diviners or soothsayers who also say many [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)fine things, but do not understand the meaning of them. And the poets appeared [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)to me to be much in the same case; and I further observed that upon the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)strength of their poetry they believed themselves to be the wisest of men [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)in other things in which they were not wise. So I departed, conceiving [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)myself to be superior to them for the same reason that I was superior to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the politicians. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> At last I went to the artisans, for I was conscious that I knew [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)nothing at all, as I may say, and I was sure that they knew many fine things; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and in this I was not mistaken, for they did know many things of which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I was ignorant, and in this they certainly were wiser than I was. But I [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)observed that even the good artisans fell into the same error as the poets; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)because they were good workmen they thought that they also knew all sorts [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of high matters, and this defect in them overshadowed their wisdom - therefore [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I asked myself on behalf of the oracle, whether I would like to be as I [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)was, neither having their knowledge nor their ignorance, or like them in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)both; and I made answer to myself and the oracle that I was better off [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)as I was. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> This investigation has led to my having many enemies of the worst [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and most dangerous kind, and has given occasion also to many calumnies, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and I am called wise, for my hearers always imagine that I myself possess [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the wisdom which I find wanting in others: but the truth is, O men of Athens, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)that God only is wise; and in this oracle he means to say that the wisdom [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of men is little or nothing; he is not speaking of Socrates, he is only [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)using my name as an illustration, as if he said, He, O men, is the wisest, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)who, like Socrates, knows that his wisdom is in truth worth nothing. And [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)so I go my way, obedient to the god, and make inquisition into the wisdom [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of anyone, whether citizen or stranger, who appears to be wise; and if [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)he is not wise, then in vindication of the oracle I show him that he is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)not wise; and this occupation quite absorbs me, and I have no time to give [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)either to any public matter of interest or to any concern of my own, but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I am in utter poverty by reason of my devotion to the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)god. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> There is another thing: - young men of the richer classes, who [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)have not much to do, come about me of their own accord; they like to hear [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the pretenders examined, and they often imitate me, and examine others [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)themselves; there are plenty of persons, as they soon enough discover, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)who think that they know something, but really know little or nothing: [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and then those who are examined by them instead of being angry with themselves [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)are angry with me: This confounded Socrates, they say; this villainous [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)misleader of youth! - and then if somebody asks them, Why, what evil does [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)he practise or teach? they do not know, and cannot tell; but in order that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)they may not appear to be at a loss, they repeat the ready-made charges [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)which are used against all philosophers about teaching things up in the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)clouds and under the earth, and having no gods, and making the worse appear [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the better cause; for they do not like to confess that their pretence of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)knowledge has been detected - which is the truth: and as they are numerous [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and ambitious and energetic, and are all in battle array and have persuasive [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)tongues, they have filled your ears with their loud and inveterate calumnies. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)And this is the reason why my three accusers, Meletus and Anytus and Lycon, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)have set upon me; Meletus, who has a quarrel with me on behalf of the poets; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)Anytus, on behalf of the craftsmen; Lycon, on behalf of the rhetoricians: [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and as I said at the beginning, I cannot expect to get rid of this mass [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of calumny all in a moment. And this, O men of Athens, is the truth and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the whole truth; I have concealed nothing, I have dissembled nothing. And [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)yet I know that this plainness of speech makes them hate me, and what is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)their hatred but a proof that I am speaking the truth? - this is the occasion [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and reason of their slander of me, as you will find out either in this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)or in any future inquiry. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> I have said enough in my defence against the first class of my [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)accusers; I turn to the second class, who are headed by Meletus, that good [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and patriotic man, as he calls himself. And now I will try to defend myself [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)against them: these new accusers must also have their affidavit read. What [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)do they say? Something of this sort: - That Socrates is a doer of evil, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and corrupter of the youth, and he does not believe in the gods of the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)state, and has other new divinities of his own. That is the sort of charge; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and now let us examine the particular counts. He says that I am a doer [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of evil, who corrupt the youth; but I say, O men of Athens, that Meletus [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)is a doer of evil, and the evil is that he makes a joke of a serious matter, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and is too ready at bringing other men to trial from a pretended zeal and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)interest about matters in which he really never had the smallest interest. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)And the truth of this I will endeavor to prove. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Come hither, Meletus, and let me ask a question of you. You think [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)a great deal about the improvement of youth? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Yes, I do. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Tell the judges, then, who is their improver; for you must know, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)as you have taken the pains to discover their corrupter, and are citing [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and accusing me before them. Speak, then, and tell the judges who their [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)improver is. Observe, Meletus, that you are silent, and have nothing to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)say. But is not this rather disgraceful, and a very considerable proof [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of what I was saying, that you have no interest in the matter? Speak up, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)friend, and tell us who their improver is. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> The laws. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> But that, my good sir, is not my meaning. I want to know who the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)person is, who, in the first place, knows the laws. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> The judges, Socrates, who are present in court. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> What do you mean to say, Meletus, that they are able to instruct [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and improve youth? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Certainly they are. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> What, all of them, or some only and not others? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> All of them. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> By the goddess Here, that is good news! There are plenty of improvers, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)then. And what do you say of the audience, - do they improve [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)them? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Yes, they do. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> And the senators? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Yes, the senators improve them. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> But perhaps the members of the citizen assembly corrupt them? - [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)or do they too improve them? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> They improve them. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Then every Athenian improves and elevates them; all with the exception [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of myself; and I alone am their corrupter? Is that what you [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)affirm? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> That is what I stoutly affirm. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> I am very unfortunate if that is true. But suppose I ask you a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)question: Would you say that this also holds true in the case of horses? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)Does one man do them harm and all the world good? Is not the exact opposite [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of this true? One man is able to do them good, or at least not many; - [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the trainer of horses, that is to say, does them good, and others who have [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)to do with them rather injure them? Is not that true, Meletus, of horses, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)or any other animals? Yes, certainly. Whether you and Anytus say yes or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)no, that is no matter. Happy indeed would be the condition of youth if [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)they had one corrupter only, and all the rest of the world were their improvers. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)And you, Meletus, have sufficiently shown that you never had a thought [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)about the young: your carelessness is seen in your not caring about matters [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)spoken of in this very indictment. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> And now, Meletus, I must ask you another question: Which is better, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)to live among bad citizens, or among good ones? Answer, friend, I say; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)for that is a question which may be easily answered. Do not the good do [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)their neighbors good, and the bad do them evil? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Certainly. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> And is there anyone who would rather be injured than benefited [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)by those who live with him? Answer, my good friend; the law requires you [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)to answer - does anyone like to be injured? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Certainly not. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> And when you accuse me of corrupting and deteriorating the youth, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)do you allege that I corrupt them intentionally or unintentionally? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Intentionally, I say. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> But you have just admitted that the good do their neighbors good, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and the evil do them evil. Now is that a truth which your superior wisdom [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)has recognized thus early in life, and am I, at my age, in such darkness [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and ignorance as not to know that if a man with whom I have to live is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)corrupted by me, I am very likely to be harmed by him, and yet I corrupt [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)him, and intentionally, too; - that is what you are saying, and of that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)you will never persuade me or any other human being. But either I do not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)corrupt them, or I corrupt them unintentionally, so that on either view [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of the case you lie. If my offence is unintentional, the law has no cognizance [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of unintentional offences: you ought to have taken me privately, and warned [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and admonished me; for if I had been better advised, I should have left [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)off doing what I only did unintentionally - no doubt I should; whereas [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)you hated to converse with me or teach me, but you indicted me in this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)court, which is a place not of instruction, but of punishment. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> I have shown, Athenians, as I was saying, that Meletus has no care [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)at all, great or small, about the matter. But still I should like to know, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)Meletus, in what I am affirmed to corrupt the young. I suppose you mean, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)as I infer from your indictment, that I teach them not to acknowledge the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)gods which the state acknowledges, but some other new divinities or spiritual [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)agencies in their stead. These are the lessons which corrupt the youth, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)as you say. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Yes, that I say emphatically. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Then, by the gods, Meletus, of whom we are speaking, tell me and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the court, in somewhat plainer terms, what you mean! for I do not as yet [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)understand whether you affirm that I teach others to acknowledge some gods, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and therefore do believe in gods and am not an entire atheist - this you [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)do not lay to my charge; but only that they are not the same gods which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the city recognizes - the charge is that they are different gods. Or, do [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)you mean to say that I am an atheist simply, and a teacher of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)atheism? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> I mean the latter - that you are a complete [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)atheist. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> That is an extraordinary statement, Meletus. Why do you say that? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)Do you mean that I do not believe in the godhead of the sun or moon, which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)is the common creed of all men? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> I assure you, judges, that he does not believe in them; for he [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)says that the sun is stone, and the moon earth. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Friend Meletus, you think that you are accusing Anaxagoras; and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)you have but a bad opinion of the judges, if you fancy them ignorant to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)such a degree as not to know that those doctrines are found in the books [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of Anaxagoras the Clazomenian, who is full of them. And these are the doctrines [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)which the youth are said to learn of Socrates, when there are not unfrequently [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)exhibitions of them at the theatre (price of admission one drachma at the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)most); and they might cheaply purchase them, and laugh at Socrates if he [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)pretends to father such eccentricities. And so, Meletus, you really think [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)that I do not believe in any god? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> I swear by Zeus that you believe absolutely in none at [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)all. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> You are a liar, Meletus, not believed even by yourself. For I cannot [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)help thinking, O men of Athens, that Meletus is reckless and impudent, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and that he has written this indictment in a spirit of mere wantonness [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and youthful bravado. Has he not compounded a riddle, thinking to try me? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)He said to himself: - I shall see whether this wise Socrates will discover [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)my ingenious contradiction, or whether I shall be able to deceive him and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the rest of them. For he certainly does appear to me to contradict himself [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)in the indictment as much as if he said that Socrates is guilty of not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)believing in the gods, and yet of believing in them - but this surely is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)a piece of fun. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> I should like you, O men of Athens, to join me in examining what [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I conceive to be his inconsistency; and do you, Meletus, answer. And I [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)must remind you that you are not to interrupt me if I speak in my accustomed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)manner. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Did ever man, Meletus, believe in the existence of human things, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and not of human beings? ... I wish, men of Athens, that he would answer, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and not be always trying to get up an interruption. Did ever any man believe [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)in horsemanship, and not in horses? or in flute-playing, and not in flute-players? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)No, my friend; I will answer to you and to the court, as you refuse to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)answer for yourself. There is no man who ever did. But now please to answer [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the next question: Can a man believe in spiritual and divine agencies, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and not in spirits or demigods? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> He cannot. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> I am glad that I have extracted that answer, by the assistance [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of the court; nevertheless you swear in the indictment that I teach and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)believe in divine or spiritual agencies (new or old, no matter for that); [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)at any rate, I believe in spiritual agencies, as you say and swear in the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)affidavit; but if I believe in divine beings, I must believe in spirits [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)or demigods; - is not that true? Yes, that is true, for I may assume that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)your silence gives assent to that. Now what are spirits or demigods? are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)they not either gods or the sons of gods? Is that true? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Yes, that is true. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> But this is just the ingenious riddle of which I was speaking: [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the demigods or spirits are gods, and you say first that I don't believe [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)in gods, and then again that I do believe in gods; that is, if I believe [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)in demigods. For if the demigods are the illegitimate sons of gods, whether [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)by the Nymphs or by any other mothers, as is thought, that, as all men [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)will allow, necessarily implies the existence of their parents. You might [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)as well affirm the existence of mules, and deny that of horses and asses. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)Such nonsense, Meletus, could only have been intended by you as a trial [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of me. You have put this into the indictment because you had nothing real [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of which to accuse me. But no one who has a particle of understanding will [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)ever be convinced by you that the same man can believe in divine and superhuman [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)things, and yet not believe that there are gods and demigods and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)heroes. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> I have said enough in answer to the charge of Meletus: any elaborate [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)defence is unnecessary; but as I was saying before, I certainly have many [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)enemies, and this is what will be my destruction if I am destroyed; of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)that I am certain; - not Meletus, nor yet Anytus, but the envy and detraction [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of the world, which has been the death of many good men, and will probably [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)be the death of many more; there is no danger of my being the last of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)them. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Someone will say: And are you not ashamed, Socrates, of a course [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of life which is likely to bring you to an untimely end? To him I may fairly [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)answer: There you are mistaken: a man who is good for anything ought not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)to calculate the chance of living or dying; he ought only to consider whether [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)in doing anything he is doing right or wrong - acting the part of a good [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)man or of a bad. Whereas, according to your view, the heroes who fell at [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)Troy were not good for much, and the son of Thetis above all, who altogether [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)despised danger in comparison with disgrace; and when his goddess mother [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)said to him, in his eagerness to slay Hector, that if he avenged his companion [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)Patroclus, and slew Hector, he would die himself - "Fate," as she said, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)"waits upon you next after Hector"; he, hearing this, utterly despised [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)danger and death, and instead of fearing them, feared rather to live in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)dishonor, and not to avenge his friend. "Let me die next," he replies, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)"and be avenged of my enemy, rather than abide here by the beaked ships, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)a scorn and a burden of the earth." Had Achilles any thought of death and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)danger? For wherever a man's place is, whether the place which he has chosen [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)or that in which he has been placed by a commander, there he ought to remain [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)in the hour of danger; he should not think of death or of anything, but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of disgrace. And this, O men of Athens, is a true saying. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Strange, indeed, would be my conduct, O men of Athens, if I who, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)when I was ordered by the generals whom you chose to command me at Potidaea [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and Amphipolis and Delium, remained where they placed me, like any other [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)man, facing death; if, I say, now, when, as I conceive and imagine, God [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)orders me to fulfil the philosopher's mission of searching into myself [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and other men, I were to desert my post through fear of death, or any other [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)fear; that would indeed be strange, and I might justly be arraigned in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)court for denying the existence of the gods, if I disobeyed the oracle [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)because I was afraid of death: then I should be fancying that I was wise [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)when I was not wise. For this fear of death is indeed the pretence of wisdom, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and not real wisdom, being the appearance of knowing the unknown; since [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)no one knows whether death, which they in their fear apprehend to be the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)greatest evil, may not be the greatest good. Is there not here conceit [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of knowledge, which is a disgraceful sort of ignorance? And this is the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)point in which, as I think, I am superior to men in general, and in which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I might perhaps fancy myself wiser than other men, - that whereas I know [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)but little of the world below, I do not suppose that I know: but I do know [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)that injustice and disobedience to a better, whether God or man, is evil [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and dishonorable, and I will never fear or avoid a possible good rather [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)than a certain evil. And therefore if you let me go now, and reject the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)counsels of Anytus, who said that if I were not put to death I ought not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)to have been prosecuted, and that if I escape now, your sons will all be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)utterly ruined by listening to my words - if you say to me, Socrates, this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)time we will not mind Anytus, and will let you off, but upon one condition, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)that are to inquire and speculate in this way any more, and that if you [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)are caught doing this again you shall die; - if this was the condition [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)on which you let me go, I should reply: Men of Athens, I honor and love [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)you; but I shall obey God rather than you, and while I have life and strength [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I shall never cease from the practice and teaching of philosophy, exhorting [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)anyone whom I meet after my manner, and convincing him, saying: O my friend, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)why do you who are a citizen of the great and mighty and wise city of Athens, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)care so much about laying up the greatest amount of money and honor and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)reputation, and so little about wisdom and truth and the greatest improvement [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of the soul, which you never regard or heed at all? Are you not ashamed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of this? And if the person with whom I am arguing says: Yes, but I do care; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I do not depart or let him go at once; I interrogate and examine and cross-examine [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)him, and if I think that he has no virtue, but only says that he has, I [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)reproach him with undervaluing the greater, and overvaluing the less. And [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)this I should say to everyone whom I meet, young and old, citizen and alien, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)but especially to the citizens, inasmuch as they are my brethren. For this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)is the command of God, as I would have you know; and I believe that to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)this day no greater good has ever happened in the state than my service [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)to the God. For I do nothing but go about persuading you all, old and young [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)alike, not to take thought for your persons and your properties, but first [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and chiefly to care about the greatest improvement of the soul. I tell [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)you that virtue is not given by money, but that from virtue come money [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and every other good of man, public as well as private. This is my teaching, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and if this is the doctrine which corrupts the youth, my influence is ruinous [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)indeed. But if anyone says that this is not my teaching, he is speaking [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)an untruth. Wherefore, O men of Athens, I say to you, do as Anytus bids [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)or not as Anytus bids, and either acquit me or not; but whatever you do, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)know that I shall never alter my ways, not even if I have to die many [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)times. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Men of Athens, do not interrupt, but hear me; there was an agreement [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)between us that you should hear me out. And I think that what I am going [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)to say will do you good: for I have something more to say, at which you [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)may be inclined to cry out; but I beg that you will not do this. I would [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)have you know that, if you kill such a one as I am, you will injure yourselves [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)more than you will injure me. Meletus and Anytus will not injure me: they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)cannot; for it is not in the nature of things that a bad man should injure [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)a better than himself. I do not deny that he may, perhaps, kill him, or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)drive him into exile, or deprive him of civil rights; and he may imagine, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and others may imagine, that he is doing him a great injury: but in that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I do not agree with him; for the evil of doing as Anytus is doing - of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)unjustly taking away another man's life - is greater far. And now, Athenians, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I am not going to argue for my own sake, as you may think, but for yours, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)that you may not sin against the God, or lightly reject his boon by condemning [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)me. For if you kill me you will not easily find another like me, who, if [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I may use such a ludicrous figure of speech, am a sort of gadfly, given [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)to the state by the God; and the state is like a great and noble steed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)who is tardy in his motions owing to his very size, and requires to be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)stirred into life. I am that gadfly which God has given the state and all [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)day long and in all places am always fastening upon you, arousing and persuading [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and reproaching you. And as you will not easily find another like me, I [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)would advise you to spare me. I dare say that you may feel irritated at [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)being suddenly awakened when you are caught napping; and you may think [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)that if you were to strike me dead, as Anytus advises, which you easily [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)might, then you would sleep on for the remainder of your lives, unless [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)God in his care of you gives you another gadfly. And that I am given to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)you by God is proved by this: - that if I had been like other men, I should [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)not have neglected all my own concerns, or patiently seen the neglect of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)them during all these years, and have been doing yours, coming to you individually, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)like a father or elder brother, exhorting you to regard virtue; this I [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)say, would not be like human nature. And had I gained anything, or if my [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)exhortations had been paid, there would have been some sense in that: but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)now, as you will perceive, not even the impudence of my accusers dares [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)to say that I have ever exacted or sought pay of anyone; they have no witness [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of that. And I have a witness of the truth of what I say; my poverty is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)a sufficient witness. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Someone may wonder why I go about in private, giving advice and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)busying myself with the concerns of others, but do not venture to come [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)forward in public and advise the state. I will tell you the reason of this. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)You have often heard me speak of an oracle or sign which comes to me, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)is the divinity which Meletus ridicules in the indictment. This sign I [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)have had ever since I was a child. The sign is a voice which comes to me [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and always forbids me to do something which I am going to do, but never [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)commands me to do anything, and this is what stands in the way of my being [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)a politician. And rightly, as I think. For I am certain, O men of Athens, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)that if I had engaged in politics, I should have perished long ago and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)done no good either to you or to myself. And don't be offended at my telling [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)you the truth: for the truth is that no man who goes to war with you or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)any other multitude, honestly struggling against the commission of unrighteousness [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and wrong in the state, will save his life; he who will really fight for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the right, if he would live even for a little while, must have a private [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)station and not a public one. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> I can give you as proofs of this, not words only, but deeds, which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)you value more than words. Let me tell you a passage of my own life, which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)will prove to you that I should never have yielded to injustice from any [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)fear of death, and that if I had not yielded I should have died at once. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I will tell you a story - tasteless, perhaps, and commonplace, but nevertheless [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)true. The only office of state which I ever held, O men of Athens, was [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)that of senator; the tribe Antiochis, which is my tribe, had the presidency [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)at the trial of the generals who had not taken up the bodies of the slain [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)after the battle of Arginusae; and you proposed to try them all together, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)which was illegal, as you all thought afterwards; but at the time I was [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the only one of the Prytanes who was opposed to the illegality, and I gave [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)my vote against you; and when the orators threatened to impeach and arrest [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)me, and have me taken away, and you called and shouted, I made up my mind [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)that I would run the risk, having law and justice with me, rather than [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)take part in your injustice because I feared imprisonment and death. This [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)happened in the days of the democracy. But when the oligarchy of the Thirty [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)was in power, they sent for me and four others into the rotunda, and bade [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)us bring Leon the Salaminian from Salamis, as they wanted to execute him. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)This was a specimen of the sort of commands which they were always giving [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)with the view of implicating as many as possible in their crimes; and then [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I showed, not in words only, but in deed, that, if I may be allowed to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)use such an expression, I cared not a straw for death, and that my only [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)fear was the fear of doing an unrighteous or unholy thing. For the strong [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)arm of that oppressive power did not frighten me into doing wrong; and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)when we came out of the rotunda the other four went to Salamis and fetched [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)Leon, but I went quietly home. For which I might have lost my life, had [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)not the power of the Thirty shortly afterwards come to an end. And to this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)many will witness. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Now do you really imagine that I could have survived all these [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)years, if I had led a public life, supposing that like a good man I had [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)always supported the right and had made justice, as I ought, the first [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)thing? No, indeed, men of Athens, neither I nor any other. But I have been [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)always the same in all my actions, public as well as private, and never [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)have I yielded any base compliance to those who are slanderously termed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)my disciples or to any other. For the truth is that I have no regular [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)disciples: but if anyone likes to come and hear me while I am pursuing [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)my mission, whether he be young or old, he may freely come. Nor do I converse [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)with those who pay only, and not with those who do not pay; but anyone, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)whether he be rich or poor, may ask and answer me and listen to my words; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and whether he turns out to be a bad man or a good one, that cannot be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)justly laid to my charge, as I never taught him anything. And if anyone [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)says that he has ever learned or heard anything from me in private which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)all the world has not heard, I should like you to know that he is speaking [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)an untruth. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> But I shall be asked, Why do people delight in continually conversing [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)with you? I have told you already, Athenians, the whole truth about this: [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)they like to hear the cross-examination of the pretenders to wisdom; there [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)is amusement in this. And this is a duty which the God has imposed upon [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)me, as I am assured by oracles, visions, and in every sort of way in which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the will of divine power was ever signified to anyone. This is true, O [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)Athenians; or, if not true, would be soon refuted. For if I am really corrupting [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the youth, and have corrupted some of them already, those of them who have [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)grown up and have become sensible that I gave them bad advice in the days [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of their youth should come forward as accusers and take their revenge; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and if they do not like to come themselves, some of their relatives, fathers, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)brothers, or other kinsmen, should say what evil their families suffered [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)at my hands. Now is their time. Many of them I see in the court. There [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)is Crito, who is of the same age and of the same deme with myself; and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)there is Critobulus his son, whom I also see. Then again there is Lysanias [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)of Sphettus, who is the father of Aeschines - he is present; and also there [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)is Antiphon of Cephisus, who is the father of Epignes; and there are the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)brothers of several who have associated with me. There is Nicostratus the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)son of Theosdotides, and the brother of Theodotus (now Theodotus himself [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)is dead, and therefore he, at any rate, will not seek to stop him); and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)there is Paralus the son of Demodocus, who had a brother Theages; and Adeimantus [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the son of Ariston, whose brother Plato is present; and Aeantodorus, who [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)is the brother of Apollodorus, whom I also see. I might mention a great [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)many others, any of whom Meletus should have produced as witnesses in the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)course of his speech; and let him still produce them, if he has forgotten [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)- I will make way for him. And let him say, if he has any testimony of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the sort which he can produce. Nay, Athenians, the very opposite is the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)truth. For all these are ready to witness on behalf of the corrupter, of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the destroyer of their kindred, as Meletus and Anytus call me; not the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)corrupted youth only - there might have been a motive for that - but their [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)uncorrupted elder relatives. Why should they too support me with their [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)testimony? Why, indeed, except for the sake of truth and justice, and because [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)they know that I am speaking the truth, and that Meletus is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)lying. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)

> Well, Athenians, this and the like of this is nearly all the defence [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)which I have to offer. Yet a word more. Perhaps there may be someone who [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)is offended at me, when he calls to mind how he himself, on a similar or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)even a less serious occasion, had recourse to prayers and supplications [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)with many tears, and how he produced his children in court, which was a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)moving spectacle, together with a posse of his relations and friends; whereas [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)I, who am probably in danger of my life, will do none of these things. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)Perhaps this may come into his mind, and he may be set against me, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)vote in anger because he is displeased at this. Now if there be such a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)person among you, which I am far from affirming, I may fairly reply to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)him: My friend, I am a man, and like other men, a creature of flesh and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)blood, and not of wood or stone, as Homer says; and I have a family, yes, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)and sons. O Athenians, three in number, one of whom is growing up, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the two others are still young; and yet I will not bring any of them hither [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)in order to petition you for an acquittal. And why not? Not from any self-will [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)or disregard of you. Whether I am or am not afraid of death is another [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)question, of which I will not now speak. But my reason simply is that I [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)feel such conduct to be discreditable to myself, and you, and the whole [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)state. One who has reached my years, and who has a name for wisdom, whether [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)deserved or not, ought not to debase himself. At any rate, the world has [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)decided that Socrates is in some way superior to other men. And if those [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)among you who are said to be superior in wisdom and courage, and any other [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)virtue, demean themselves in this way, how shameful is their conduct! I [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)have seen men of reputation, when they have been condemned, behaving in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)the strangest manner: they seemed to fancy that they were going to suffer [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)something dreadful if they died, and that they could be immortal if you [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)only allowed them to live; and I think that they were a dishonor to the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)state, and that any stranger coming in would say of them that the most [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)eminent men of Athens, to whom the Athenians themselves give honor and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/apology.html)command, are no better than women.
