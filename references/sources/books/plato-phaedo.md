**Echecrates.** Were you yourself, Phaedo, in the prison with [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)Socrates on the day when he drank the poison? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Phaedo.** Yes, Echecrates, I was. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Ech.** I wish that you would tell me about his death. What [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)did he say in his last hours? We were informed that he died by taking poison, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)but no one knew anything more; for no Phliasian ever goes to Athens now, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)and a long time has elapsed since any Athenian found his way to Phlius, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)and therefore we had no clear account. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Phaed.** Did you not hear of the proceedings at the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)trial? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Ech.** Yes; someone told us about the trial, and we could [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)not understand why, having been condemned, he was put to death, as appeared, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)not at the time, but long afterwards. What was the reason of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)this? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Phaed.** An accident, Echecrates. The reason was that the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)stern of the ship which the Athenians send to Delos happened to have been [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)crowned on the day before he was tried. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Ech.** What is this ship? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Phaed.** This is the ship in which, as the Athenians say, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)Theseus went to Crete when he took with him the fourteen youths, and was [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)the saviour of them and of himself. And they were said to have vowed to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)Apollo at the time, that if they were saved they would make an annual pilgrimage [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)to Delos. Now this custom still continues, and the whole period of the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)voyage to and from Delos, beginning when the priest of Apollo crowns the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)stern of the ship, is a holy season, during which the city is not allowed [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)to be polluted by public executions; and often, when the vessel is detained [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)by adverse winds, there may be a very considerable delay. As I was saying, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)the ship was crowned on the day before the trial, and this was the reason [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)why Socrates lay in prison and was not put to death until long after he [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)was condemned. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Ech.** What was the manner of his death, Phaedo? What was [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)said or done? And which of his friends had he with him? Or were they not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)allowed by the authorities to be present? And did he die [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)alone? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Phaed.** No; there were several of his friends with [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)him. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Ech.** If you have nothing to do, I wish that you would tell [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)me what passed, as exactly as you can. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Phaed.** I have nothing to do, and will try to gratify your [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)wish. For to me, too, there is no greater pleasure than to have Socrates [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)brought to my recollection, whether I speak myself or hear another speak [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)of him. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Ech.** You will have listeners who are of the same mind with [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)you, and I hope that you will be as exact as you can. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Phaed.** I remember the strange feeling which came over me [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)at being with him. For I could hardly believe that I was present at the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)death of a friend, and therefore I did not pity him, Echecrates; his mien [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)and his language were so noble and fearless in the hour of death that to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)me he appeared blessed. I thought that in going to the other world he could [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)not be without a divine call, and that he would be happy, if any man ever [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)was, when he arrived there, and therefore I did not pity him as might seem [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)natural at such a time. But neither could I feel the pleasure which I usually [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)felt in philosophical discourse (for philosophy was the theme of which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)we spoke). I was pleased, and I was also pained, because I knew that he [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)was soon to die, and this strange mixture of feeling was shared by us all; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)we were laughing and weeping by turns, especially the excitable Apollodorus-you [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)know the sort of man? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Ech.** Yes. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Phaed.** He was quite overcome; and I myself and all of us [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)were greatly moved. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Ech.** Who were present? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Phaed.** Of native Athenians there were, besides Apollodorus, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)Critobulus and his father Crito, Hermogenes, Epigenes, Aeschines, and Antisthenes; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)likewise Ctesippus of the deme of Paeania, Menexenus, and some others; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)but Plato, if I am not mistaken, was ill. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Ech.** Were there any strangers? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Phaed.** Yes, there were; Simmias the Theban, and Cebes, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)Phaedondes; Euclid and Terpison, who came from Megara. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Ech.** And was Aristippus there, and Cleombrotus? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Phaed.** No, they were said to be in Aegina. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Ech.** Anyone else? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Phaed.** I think that these were about [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)all. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Ech.** And what was the discourse of which you [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)spoke? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

**Phaed.** I will begin at the beginning, and endeavor to repeat [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)the entire conversation. You must understand that we had been previously [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)in the habit of assembling early in the morning at the court in which the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)trial was held, and which is not far from the prison. There we remained [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)talking with one another until the opening of the prison doors (for they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)were not opened very early), and then went in and generally passed the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)day with Socrates. On the last morning the meeting was earlier than usual; [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)this was owing to our having heard on the previous evening that the sacred [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)ship had arrived from Delos, and therefore we agreed to meet very early [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)at the accustomed place. On our going to the prison, the jailer who answered [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)the door, instead of admitting us, came out and bade us wait and he would [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)call us. "For the Eleven," he said, "are now with Socrates; they are taking [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)off his chains, and giving orders that he is to die to-day." He soon returned [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)and said that we might come in. On entering we found Socrates just released [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)from chains, and Xanthippe, whom you know, sitting by him, and holding [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)his child in her arms. When she saw us she uttered a cry and said, as women [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)will: "O Socrates, this is the last time that either you will converse [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)with your friends, or they with you." Socrates turned to Crito and said: [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)"Crito, let someone take her home." Some of Crito's people accordingly [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)led her away, crying out and beating herself. And when she was gone, Socrates, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)sitting up on the couch, began to bend and rub his leg, saying, as he rubbed: [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)"How singular is the thing called pleasure, and how curiously related to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)pain, which might be thought to be the opposite of it; for they never come [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)to a man together, and yet he who pursues either of them is generally compelled [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)to take the other. They are two, and yet they grow together out of one [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)head or stem; and I cannot help thinking that if Aesop had noticed them, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)he would have made a fable about God trying to reconcile their strife, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)and when he could not, he fastened their heads together; and this is the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)reason why when one comes the other follows, as I find in my own case pleasure [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)comes following after the pain in my leg, which was caused by the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)chain." [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Upon this Cebes said: I am very glad indeed, Socrates, that you [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)mentioned the name of Aesop. For that reminds me of a question which has [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)been asked by others, and was asked of me only the day before yesterday [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)by Evenus the poet, and as he will be sure to ask again, you may as well [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)tell me what I should say to him, if you would like him to have an answer. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)He wanted to know why you who never before wrote a line of poetry, now [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)that you are in prison are putting Aesop into verse, and also composing [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)that hymn in honor of Apollo. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Tell him, Cebes, he replied, that I had no idea of rivalling him [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)or his poems; which is the truth, for I knew that I could not do that. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)But I wanted to see whether I could purge away a scruple which I felt about [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)certain dreams. In the course of my life I have often had intimations in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)dreams "that I should make music." The same dream came to me sometimes [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)in one form, and sometimes in another, but always saying the same or nearly [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)the same words: Make and cultivate music, said the dream. And hitherto [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)I had imagined that this was only intended to exhort and encourage me in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)the study of philosophy, which has always been the pursuit of my life, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)and is the noblest and best of music. The dream was bidding me to do what [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)I was already doing, in the same way that the competitor in a race is bidden [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)by the spectators to run when he is already running. But I was not certain [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)of this, as the dream might have meant music in the popular sense of the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)word, and being under sentence of death, and the festival giving me a respite, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)I thought that I should be safer if I satisfied the scruple, and, in obedience [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)to the dream, composed a few verses before I departed. And first I made [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)a hymn in honor of the god of the festival, and then considering that a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)poet, if he is really to be a poet or maker, should not only put words [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)together but make stories, and as I have no invention, I took some fables [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)of esop, which I had ready at hand and knew, and turned them into verse. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)Tell Evenus this, and bid him be of good cheer; that I would have him come [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)after me if he be a wise man, and not tarry; and that to-day I am likely [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)to be going, for the Athenians say that I must. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Simmias said: What a message for such a man! having been a frequent [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)companion of his, I should say that, as far as I know him, he will never [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)take your advice unless he is obliged. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Why, said Socrates,-is not Evenus a philosopher? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

I think that he is, said Simmias. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Then he, or any man who has the spirit of philosophy, will be willing [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)to die, though he will not take his own life, for that is held not to be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)right. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Here he changed his position, and put his legs off the couch on [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)to the ground, and during the rest of the conversation he remained [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)sitting. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Why do you say, inquired Cebes, that a man ought not to take his [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)own life, but that the philosopher will be ready to follow the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)dying? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Socrates replied: And have you, Cebes and Simmias, who are acquainted [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)with Philolaus, never heard him speak of this? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

I never understood him, Socrates. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

My words, too, are only an echo; but I am very willing to say what [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)I have heard: and indeed, as I am going to another place, I ought to be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)thinking and talking of the nature of the pilgrimage which I am about to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)make. What can I do better in the interval between this and the setting [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)of the sun? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Then tell me, Socrates, why is suicide held not to be right? as [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)I have certainly heard Philolaus affirm when he was staying with us at [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)Thebes: and there are others who say the same, although none of them has [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)ever made me understand him. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

But do your best, replied Socrates, and the day may come when you [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)will understand. I suppose that you wonder why, as most things which are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)evil may be accidentally good, this is to be the only exception (for may [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)not death, too, be better than life in some cases?), and why, when a man [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)is better dead, he is not permitted to be his own benefactor, but must [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)wait for the hand of another. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

By Jupiter! yes, indeed, said Cebes, laughing, and speaking in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)his native Doric. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

I admit the appearance of inconsistency, replied Socrates, but [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)there may not be any real inconsistency after all in this. There is a doctrine [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)uttered in secret that man is a prisoner who has no right to open the door [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)of his prison and run away; this is a great mystery which I do not quite [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)understand. Yet I, too, believe that the gods are our guardians, and that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)we are a possession of theirs. Do you not agree? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Yes, I agree to that, said Cebes. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And if one of your own possessions, an ox or an ass, for example took [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)the liberty of putting himself out of the way when you had given no intimation [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)of your wish that he should die, would you not be angry with him, and would [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)you not punish him if you could? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Certainly, replied Cebes. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Then there may be reason in saying that a man should wait, and not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)take his own life until God summons him, as he is now summoning [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)me. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Yes, Socrates, said Cebes, there is surely reason in that. And [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)yet how can you reconcile this seemingly true belief that God is our guardian [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)and we his possessions, with that willingness to die which we were attributing [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)to the philosopher? That the wisest of men should be willing to leave this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)service in which they are ruled by the gods who are the best of rulers [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)is not reasonable, for surely no wise man thinks that when set at liberty [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)he can take better care of himself than the gods take of him. A fool may [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)perhaps think this-he may argue that he had better run away from his master, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)not considering that his duty is to remain to the end, and not to run away [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)from the good, and that there is no sense in his running away. But the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)wise man will want to be ever with him who is better than himself. Now [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)this, Socrates, is the reverse of what was just now said; for upon this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)view the wise man should sorrow and the fool rejoice at passing out of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)life. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

The earnestness of Cebes seemed to please Socrates. Here, said [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)he, turning to us, is a man who is always inquiring, and is not to be convinced [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)all in a moment, nor by every argument. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And in this case, added Simmias, his objection does appear to me [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)to have some force. For what can be the meaning of a truly wise man wanting [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)to fly away and lightly leave a master who is better than himself? And [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)I rather imagine that Cebes is referring to you; he thinks that you are [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)too ready to leave us, and too ready to leave the gods who, as you acknowledge, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)are our good rulers. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Yes, replied Socrates; there is reason in that. And this indictment [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)you think that I ought to answer as if I were in court? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

That is what we should like, said Simmias. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Then I must try to make a better impression upon you than I did when [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)defending myself before the judges. For I am quite ready to acknowledge, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)Simmias and Cebes, that I ought to be grieved at death, if I were not persuaded [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)that I am going to other gods who are wise and good (of this I am as certain [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)as I can be of anything of the sort) and to men departed (though I am not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)so certain of this), who are better than those whom I leave behind; and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)therefore I do not grieve as I might have done, for I have good hope that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)there is yet something remaining for the dead, and, as has been said of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)old, some far better thing for the good than for the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)evil. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

But do you mean to take away your thoughts with you, Socrates? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)said Simmias. Will you not communicate them to us?-the benefit is one in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)which we too may hope to share. Moreover, if you succeed in convincing [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)us, that will be an answer to the charge against yourself. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Certainly not, answered Simmias. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And what do you say of the pleasures of love-should he care about [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)them? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

By no means. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And will he think much of the other ways of indulging the body-for [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)example, the acquisition of costly raiment, or sandals, or other adornments [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)of the body? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

I should say the true philosopher would despise [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)them. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Would you not say that he is entirely concerned with the soul and not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)with the body? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Certainly, he replied. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Then when does the soul attain truth?-for in attempting to consider [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)anything in company with the body she is obviously deceived. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Then must not existence be revealed to her in thought, if at [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)all? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Yes. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And thought is best when the mind is gathered into herself and none [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)of these things trouble her-neither sounds nor sights nor pain nor any [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)pleasure-when she has as little as possible to do with the body, and has [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)no bodily sense or feeling, but is aspiring after being? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And in this the philosopher dishonors the body; his soul runs away [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)from the body and desires to be alone and by herself? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Well, but there is another thing, Simmias: Is there or is there not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)an absolute justice? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Assuredly there is. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And an absolute beauty and absolute good? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Of course. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

But did you ever behold any of them with your eyes? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Certainly not. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Or did you ever reach them with any other bodily sense? Has the reality of them [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)ever been perceived by you through the bodily organs? or rather, is not [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)the nearest approach to the knowledge of their several natures made by [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)him who so orders his intellectual vision as to have the most exact conception [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)of the essence of that which he considers? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Certainly. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And he attains to the knowledge of them in their highest purity who [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)goes to each of them with the mind alone, not allowing when in the act [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)of thought the intrusion or introduction of sight or any other sense in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)the company of reason, but with the very light of the mind in her clearness [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)penetrates into the very fight of truth in each; he has got rid, as far [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)as he can, of eyes and ears and of the whole body, which he conceives of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)only as a disturbing element, hindering the soul from the acquisition of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)knowledge when in company with her-is not this the sort of man who, if [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)ever man did, is likely to attain the knowledge of existence? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

There is admirable truth in that, Socrates, replied [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)Simmias. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And when they consider all this, must not true philosophers make a [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)reflection, of which they will speak to one another in such words as these: [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)We have found, they will say, a path of speculation which seems to bring [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)us and the argument to the conclusion that while we are in the body, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)while the soul is mingled with this mass of evil, our desire will not be [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)satisfied, and our desire is of the truth. For [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)no impure thing is allowed to approach the pure. These are the sort of [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)words, Simmias, which the true lovers of wisdom cannot help saying to one [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)another, and thinking. You will agree with me in that? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Certainly, Socrates. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

But if this is true, O my friend, then there is great hope that, going [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)whither I go, I shall there be satisfied with that which has been the chief [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)concern of you and me in our past lives. And now that the hour of departure [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)is appointed to me, this is the hope with which I depart, and not I only, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)but every man who believes that he has his mind purified. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Certainly, replied Simmias. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And what is purification but the separation of the soul from the body, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)as I was saying before; the habit of the soul gathering and collecting [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)herself into herself, out of all the courses of the body; the dwelling [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)in her own place alone, as in another life, so also in this, as far as [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)she can; the release of the soul from the chains of the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)body? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Very true, he said. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And what is that which is termed death, but this very separation and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)release of the soul from the body? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

To be sure, he said. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And the true philosophers, and they only, study and are eager to release [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)the soul. Is not the separation and release of the soul from the body their [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)especial study? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And as I was saying at first, there would be a ridiculous contradiction [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)in men studying to live as nearly as they can in a state of death, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)yet repining when death comes. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Then, Simmias, as the true philosophers are ever studying death, to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)them, of all men, death is the least terrible. Look at the matter in this [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)way: how inconsistent of them to have been always enemies of the body, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)and wanting to have the soul alone, and when this is granted to them, to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)be trembling and repining; instead of rejoicing at their departing to that [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)place where, when they arrive, they hope to gain that which in life they [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)loved (and this was wisdom), and at the same time to be rid of the company [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)of their enemy. Many a man has been willing to go to the world below in [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)the hope of seeing there an earthly love, or wife, or son, and conversing [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)with them. And [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)if this be true, he would be very absurd, as I was saying, if he were to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)fear death. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

He would, indeed, replied Simmias. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And when you see a man who is repining at the approach of death, is [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)not his reluctance a sufficient proof that he is not a lover of wisdom, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)but a lover of the body, and probably at the same time a lover of either [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)money or power, or both? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

That is very true, he replied. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

There is a virtue, Simmias, which is named courage. Is not that a special [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)attribute of the philosopher? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Again, there is temperance. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

That is not to be denied. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

For the courage and temperance of other men, if you will consider them, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)are really a contradiction. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

How is that, Socrates? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Well, he said, you are aware that death is regarded by men in general [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)as a great evil. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

That is true, he said. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And do not courageous men endure death because they are afraid of yet [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)greater evils? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Then all but the philosophers are courageous only from fear, and because [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)they are afraid; and yet that a man should be courageous from fear, and [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)because he is a coward, is surely a strange thing. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Very true. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And are not the temperate exactly in the same case? And that is what I mean by saying that they are temperate [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)through intemperance. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

That appears to be true. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Yet the exchange of one fear or pleasure or pain for another fear or [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)pleasure or pain, which are measured like coins, the greater with the less, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)is not the exchange of virtue. O my dear Simmias, is there not one true [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)coin for which all things ought to exchange?-and that is wisdom; and only [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)in exchange for this, and in company with this, is anything truly bought [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)or sold, whether courage or temperance or justice. And is not all true [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)virtue the companion of wisdom, no matter what fears or pleasures or other [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)similar goods or evils may or may not attend her? Death, he answered. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Quite agreed. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Then suppose that you analyze life and death to me in the same manner. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)Is not death opposed to life? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And they are generated one from the other? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

What is generated from life? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Death. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And what from death? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Then the living, whether things or persons, Cebes, are generated from [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)the dead? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

That is clear, he replied. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Then the inference is, that our souls are in the world [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)below? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Surely, he said. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And may not the other be inferred as the complement of nature, who [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)is not to be supposed to go on one leg only? And if not, a corresponding [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)process of generation in death must also be assigned to [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)her? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And what is that process? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Revival. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

And revival, if there be such a thing, is the birth of the dead into [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)the world of the living? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Quite true. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Then there is a new way in which we arrive at the inference that the [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)living come from the dead, just as the dead come from the living; and if [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)this is true, then the souls of the dead must be in some place out of which [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)they come again. And this, as I think, has been satisfactorily [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)proved. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Yes, Socrates, he said; all this seems to flow necessarily out [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)of our previous admissions. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

There is no escape from that, Socrates, said Cebes; and I think [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)that what you say is entirely true. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

Yes, he said, Cebes, I entirely think so, too; and we are not walking [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)in a vain imagination; but I am confident in the belief that there truly [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)is such a thing as living again, and that the living spring from the dead, [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)and that the souls of the dead are in existence, and that the good souls [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)have a better portion than the evil. [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

What do you mean? [](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)

[](https://classics.mit.edu/Plato/phaedo.html)
